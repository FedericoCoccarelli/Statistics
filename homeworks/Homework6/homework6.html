1- Try to explain in your own words, the concept of population and sampling distribution

In statistics, a population is the pool of individuals from which a statistical sample is drawn for a study. Thus, any selection of individuals grouped by a common feature can be said to be a population. A sample may also refer to a statistically significant portion of a population, not an entire population. For this reason, a statistical analysis of a sample must report the approximate standard deviation, or standard error, of its results from the entire population.
If the entire population is too large to be analysed, statistics tries to learn something about it by choosing and then analyzing subgroups. A subset of the population is called a "sample". Samples to be representative of the population must be chosen completely at random. The information obtained from the statistical sample allows statisticians to develop hypotheses about the larger population using the inferential statistics.

A sampling distribution is a probability distribution of a statistic obtained from a larger number of samples drawn from a specific population. The sampling distribution of a given population is the distribution of frequencies of a range of different outcomes that could possibly occur for a statistic of a population.

2- Show the expected value and variance of the sample mean and take a look at the analogous results for the sample variance

In the previous research we talked about samples, in this research we will talk about it's mean and variance called respectively sample mean and sample variance and are theirselves two random variables, we can compute their expected value and variance .

Sample mean
Let a sequence of identical distributed and indipendent random variables$X_1 , X_2 ,..., X_n$ be a random sample of size $n$ from a distribution (population), we can define the mean random variable called “sample mean” as the following: $$ \bar{X} = \frac{X_1 + X_2 + ... + X_n}{n} $$ Expected value
Starting with the definition of the sample mean, we have: $$E(\bar{X})=E(\frac{X_1+X_2+\dots+X_n}{n})$$ Then, using the linear operator property of expectation, we get: $$E(\bar{X})=\frac{1}{n} [E(X_1)+E(X_2)+\dots+E(X_n)]$$ Now, the $X_i$ are identically distributed, which means they have the same mean (\mu). Therefore, replacing (E(X_i)) with the alternative notation (\mu), we get: $$E(\bar{X})=\frac{1}{n}[\mu+\mu+\cdots+\mu]$$ Now, because there are (n) $\mu$'s in the above formula, we can rewrite the expected value as: $$E(\bar{X})=\frac{1}{n}[n \mu]=\mu $$ We have shown that the expected value of the sample mean $\bar{X}$ is $\mu$. This result is the same as the entire population.

Variance
Starting with the definition of the sample mean, we have: $$Var(\bar{X})=Var(\frac{X_1+X_2+\cdots+X_n}{n})$$ Rewriting the term on the right so that it is clear that we have a linear combination of $X_i$'s, we get: $$Var(\bar{X})=Var(\frac{1}{n}X_1+\frac{1}{n}X_2+\cdots+\frac{1}{n}X_n)$$ Then, applying the theorem on the last page, we get: $$Var(\bar{X})=\frac{1}{n^2}Var(X_1)+\frac{1}{n^2}Var(X_2)+\cdots+\frac{1}{n^2}Var(X_n)$$ Now, the $X_i$ are identically distributed, which means they have the same variance $\sigma^2$. Therefore, replacing $Var(X_i)$ with the alternative notation $\sigma^2$, we get: $$Var(\bar{X})=\frac{1}{n^2}[\sigma^2+\sigma^2+\cdots+\sigma^2]$$ Now, because there are (n) $\sigma^2$'s in the above formula, we can rewrite the expected value as: $$Var(\bar{X})=\frac{1}{n^2}[n\sigma^2]=\frac{\sigma^2}{n}$$ Our result indicates that while the expected value of the sample mean is identical to the expected value of the population, the variance of the sample mean is the variance of the population divided by the sample size.

Sample variance
As for the sample mean let's take a look at the definition of sample variance: $$[S^2 = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n}(X_i - \bar{X})^2] $$

Expected value
$$E[S^2]=\frac{1}{n-1}E[\sum(X_i^2-n\bar{X}^2)]=\frac{1}{n-1}[\sum(E(X_i^2)-nE(\bar{X}^2))]=$$ $$=\frac{1}{n-1}[\sum((Var(X_i)+E(X_i)^2)-n(Var\bar{X})+E(\bar{X}^2))]=$$ $$=\frac{1}{n-1}[\sum((\sigma^2+\mu^2)-n(\frac{\sigma^2}{n}+\mu^2))]= \frac{1}{n-1}[n(\sigma^2+\mu^2)-\sigma^2-n\mu^2]=$$ $$=\frac{1}{n-1}[(n-1)\sigma^2]=\sigma^2$$ Then the expected value of the sample variance perfectly extimates the variance of the entire population.

Variance

The variance is: $$\mbox{Var}(S^2)={\mu_4\over n}-{\sigma^4\,(n-3)\over n\,(n-1)}.$$ Here $\mu_4=\mathbb{E}[(X-\mu)^4]$ is the fourth central moment of $X$.
